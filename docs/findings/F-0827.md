## Finding

The editorial-review checklist in `version-bump-step2.md` enumerates 37 items: items `(a)`–`(e)` are MUST-level and items `(f)`–`(ak)` are SHOULD-level — 32 SHOULD-level items in total. 31 of those 32 items (every SHOULD item except `(i)`, whose escalation routes through `Provider error mapping`'s *Re-validation gate* instead) close with one and the same escalation paragraph, varying only in the contract-type noun:

> *This obligation is SHOULD-level only at loom 1.0; it escalates to a build-time pin (on the same footing as the seven-capability literal-read assertion under step 2(a) above) once Pi exposes a typed `<X>` contract that step 2(a)'s surface-inventory probe can mechanically verify, at which point the editorial audit prescribed here is superseded by the mechanical gate.*

Reviewer-side this is straight duplication: the policy is the same for all 31 items, the only per-item content is the noun naming the typed contract. The page is already 84 KB (well past the 30 KB recommended cap pinned at the top of the spec corpus) and the boilerplate accounts for a sizeable fraction of that surface; the duplication also makes the policy itself harder to evolve, because any future wording change to the escalation policy has to be edited 31 times in lock-step (or the items silently drift apart).

Independently — and this is the second half of the finding — the 32 SHOULD items are presented as a flat list at equal weight, with no priority/tier marking, no per-subsystem grouping, and no guidance for an auditor working under time pressure. The MUST/SHOULD split tells the auditor which items block the bump and which do not, but it does not differentiate, within the SHOULD set, items whose silent regression has broad blast radius (e.g. `(g)` slash-handler promise lifecycle, `(v)` turn-lifecycle event delivery, `(ab)` `complete()` cancellation honouring) from items whose blast radius is narrow (e.g. `(ae)` privilege-scoping facet drift detector, `(s)` settings write-back). Under the "MUST record one of `pass`/`fail`/`N/A`/`not-performed` for every item" rule the preamble pins, the auditor can decline to audit any individual SHOULD item by recording `not-performed`, but the document gives no guidance about *which* `not-performed` records are safer than others.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** multiple

The finding bundles two independent obligations: (A) extract the duplicated escalation policy to a single statement and delete the per-item copies, and (B) add a priority/tier or per-subsystem layer to the SHOULD list. They edit different surfaces and have different cost/value profiles; resolve them sequentially rather than as one bundle.

### Option A — Extract the escalation policy to a shared preamble and delete the 31 per-item copies

**Approach.** In `version-bump-step2.md`, immediately after the existing *Editorial-review checklist for unpinned host presuppositions.* preamble paragraph (which already states the MUST/SHOULD partition and the recording shape), add one named paragraph — call it the *Default SHOULD-item escalation policy* — that states the escalation rule once, with `<typed-contract-noun>` as a placeholder, and states that the policy applies to every item in `(f)`–`(ak)` except those whose item body explicitly names a different escalation route. From each of the 31 affected items, delete the trailing escalation sentence; leave the item body that names the typed-contract noun untouched (the items already name the contract type as part of their per-item audit-obligation prose, so the placeholder resolution stays well-defined). Item `(i)`'s existing custom escalation sentence ("wiring the fixture re-run into the bump procedure as a mechanical CI gate is the post-loom 1.0.0 maintenance follow-up noted under the *Re-validation gate* in **Provider error mapping** above") stays in place and is named as the explicit exception in the shared preamble.

**Spec edits.** One new preamble paragraph in `version-bump-step2.md`; 31 trailing-sentence deletions, one per item in `(f)`–`(ak)` excluding `(i)`; one explicit "exception: item (i)" mention in the shared preamble.

**Pros.** Mechanical, locally verifiable (grep the file for the escalation sentence after the edit and confirm zero matches). Cuts a sizeable fraction of the 84 KB page. Eliminates the lock-step hazard for any future policy reword. Preserves every audit obligation exactly as-is.

**Cons.** Per-item reading requires one extra cross-reference back to the preamble. (Mitigated: the preamble is at the top of the same short file.)

**Risks.** A future contributor adding a new SHOULD item could forget that the escalation rule is inherited and either re-copy the paragraph (re-introducing the duplication) or leave the item without any escalation framing. The shared-preamble paragraph should explicitly state that new SHOULD items inherit the default policy unless they name an alternative — same shape as the existing closing "Any future presupposition added to this page … MUST be added to this checklist in the same edit" paragraph.

### Option B — Add a priority/tier annotation to the SHOULD list

**Approach.** Either (i) prepend each `(f)`–`(ak)` item title with a one-token blast-radius tag (`[broad]` / `[moderate]` / `[narrow]`) consistent with the same `Factors:` taxonomy this review file uses, or (ii) introduce a per-subsystem grouping (e.g. *Lifecycle and cancellation*, *Provider behaviour*, *Settings and registration*, *Type and surface shape*) that re-sorts the SHOULD items under named subheadings. Either form gives a time-pressured auditor a signal about where to spend the limited audit budget.

**Spec edits.** Either per-item tag prefixes on 32 items, or new subsection headings plus a one-time re-ordering of the items.

**Pros.** Makes the "MUST record `not-performed`" escape hatch more honestly usable: an auditor who skips items deliberately can prioritise the broad-blast-radius ones. Surfaces the structural shape of the SHOULD set, which is currently invisible.

**Cons.** The blast-radius judgement is editorial and not currently anchored anywhere normative in the corpus — picking the labels is a design decision that invites bikeshedding. Sub-subsystem grouping breaks the lexicographic `(f)`–`(ak)` ordering that several other documents (notably `V18c`'s *Ships when* and `V18d`'s revert path) reference. Any later item addition would have to choose a slot in the grouping.

**Risks.** A grouping the contributor disagrees with at audit time may reduce trust in the document rather than help. A tag the spec does not back with a clear criterion can drift between contributors. This is the more contestable half of the finding and is genuinely advisory.

### Recommendation

Apply **Option A first**, as a self-contained edit. It is mechanical, scope-bounded, and free of editorial judgement; the resulting baseline is smaller and cleaner, which makes the question Option B is trying to answer easier to reason about. Defer **Option B** as a separate decision, and only land it if a named owner decides the triage layer is worth the editorial cost — `cosmetic` severity does not by itself justify the design work Option B requires. Sequencing Option A before Option B also lets a later `spec-diff-fix-loop` run against Option A's much smaller diff surface independently of any disagreement about the triage scheme. Edge case the implementer of Option A must watch: when the trailing escalation sentence is deleted from a per-item body, do not leave the item body ending mid-clause — several items embed the typed-contract noun in a phrase that the trailing escalation sentence completes; confirm each item still reads as a grammatical paragraph in isolation.

## Consequence

**Severity:** cosmetic

The escalation rule is correctly stated in every copy, so there is no risk of an auditor following the wrong policy on any individual item. The cost is editorial: page size, lock-step edit hazard for any future policy reword, and an unstructured SHOULD list that gives time-pressured auditors no signposting. No observer is affected at runtime.
