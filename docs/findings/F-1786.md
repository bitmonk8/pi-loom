## Finding

Every inbound cross-reference to the non-Error-throw coercion rule uses the link text `underlying-error string` while the target paragraph it lands on is headed **Underlying-error coercion** and is reached via the anchor `#underlying-error-coercion`. The link text names the *output* (the coerced string); the destination heading and anchor name the *process* (the coercion). The destination paragraph's own prose never uses the noun phrase the inbound links promise — it says "the underlying string" and "this guaranteed string" — so the term `underlying-error string` exists only as hyperlink display text, defined nowhere as a heading or term.

The links resolve correctly (they land on the right paragraph), but a reader who follows one and then searches `placeholder-rendering-b.md` for the heading or term "underlying-error string" finds nothing. The inbound references therefore read as introducing terminology the destination does not own.

The defect is wider than the three registry files named in the original report. The `[underlying-error string](…#underlying-error-coercion)` link form appears at six sites: `code-registry-load.md` (two rows), `code-registry-host.md`, `code-registry-runtime.md` (preamble), `pi-integration-contract/extension-bootstrap-and-per-loom.md`, and `pi-integration-contract/host-interfaces-core.md`. All point at the single anchor `#underlying-error-coercion`.

## Issue introduction

**Verdict:** present-since-inception
**Introducing commits:** `bf85092` — *pi-loom spec: resolve "non-Error-throw coercion + session_start pi.getCommands() failure contract"* (2026-06-06)
**History:** Commit `bf85092` simultaneously introduced the `#underlying-error-coercion` anchor and the §6 paragraph at the destination (whose prose uses "the underlying string") and the inbound noun-phrase link text "underlying-error string" at every consumer site that uses it today (`code-registry-runtime.md` preamble, `code-registry-load.md` row, `code-registry-host.md` row, `host-interfaces-core.md` outcome-routing summary, `extension-bootstrap-and-per-loom.md` wrap-up paragraph). The later commit `62d49d6` (2026-06-22) extended the producer-site enumeration in §6 to cover `details.cause` and added the same noun-phrase link text at `capability-probe.md`'s self-failure paragraph, preserving the mismatch rather than introducing it. The defect is therefore present-since-inception of the coercion paragraph itself: the name mismatch between the inbound link text and the destination heading/body has existed in every commit the §6 anchor has.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Add a single equivalence sentence to the §6 *Underlying-error coercion* paragraph in `docs/spec_topics/diagnostics/placeholder-rendering-b.md`, immediately after the two-step coercion list and before the existing "The truncation rule below then operates on this guaranteed string …" sentence, defining the inbound term as the coercion's output — e.g. *"The string this coercion produces is the value's **underlying-error string**."* This makes the term `underlying-error string` a defined term at the destination, so all six inbound links land on prose that names what they promised.

Keep the anchor `#underlying-error-coercion` unchanged — it is a stable target with multiple inbound references, and the process/result distinction the two names express is real (the paragraph performs the *coercion*; the *underlying-error string* is its result). Do not rewrite the inbound link texts: their noun-phrase form ("the throw's underlying-error string", `<underlying-error string truncated>`) reads naturally at every consumer site, and rewriting six occurrences across five files to "underlying-error coercion" both reads awkwardly and is unnecessary once the destination defines the term.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

A reader following one of the six inbound links lands on a paragraph that never names the term the link promised, so searching the destination for "underlying-error string" by heading or defined term fails. Pure wayfinding/terminology friction; no runtime behaviour or implementation decision diverges.
