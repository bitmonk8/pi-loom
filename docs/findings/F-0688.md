## Finding

The `LoomRegistry` drain-state contract uses two names for the same underlying value: the internal field is `drainStateTag` (type `"shutting-down" | "degraded-needs-reload" | undefined`), while the snapshot key returned from `readDrainState()` is the shorter `tag` (so dispatch sites read `snapshot.tag === "shutting-down"` rather than `snapshot.drainStateTag === "shutting-down"`). The asymmetry is deliberate, but the *Fields* paragraph introduces `drainStateTag` and proceeds to enumerate every state-transition writer (`initDrainStateTag`, `markRuntimeDegraded`) before the reader is told the snapshot exposes the same field under a different key.

The disambiguation sentence ("The internal field is named `drainStateTag`; the public snapshot key on `readDrainState`'s return value is the shorter `tag`. The rename is intentional …") only appears mid-`Methods` paragraph, after the writer methods have been listed and before `readDrainState` itself is specified. A reader who scans the *Fields* paragraph and then jumps to the `LoomRegistry.readDrainState` bullet to learn the dispatch API sees both names in use without the bridge: the `Methods` opener references "the closed `drainStateTag` value set," the writer bullets use `drainStateTag`, and the `readDrainState` signature emits `tag` — with the explanation that they refer to one field tucked into a long sentence about which sibling method names would have to be swept on a unification.

The same asymmetry then propagates loosely through neighbouring prose: the *idempotent* clause alternates between `snapshot.tag`, the bare noun "drain-state tag" (with hyphen, referring to the field abstractly), and the literal field name. None of these usages is wrong, but together they invite a reader who has not yet found the mid-`Methods` disambiguation to ask whether the snapshot really mirrors the field or carries a derived value.

The naming asymmetry was therefore baked in with the contract itself, and the placement of the disambiguation mid-`Methods` (rather than at the *Fields* entry point) has been the contract's shape from day one. No commit in the file's history has hoisted the disambiguation forward or unified the names.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Hoist a one-sentence disambiguation to the head of the *Fields* paragraph, immediately after the field-tuple enumeration is introduced and before `drainStateTag` is named, so a reader meets both names paired before either is used in a writer/reader contract. Concrete form:

> *Fields.* The contract carries two fields, exposed to readers as the snapshot tuple `{ drained, tag }` that `readDrainState()` returns (`tag` is the snapshot key for the field the rest of this section names `drainStateTag`; the rename is intentional and out of scope for loom 1.0 to unify — see *Methods* below for the full rationale). (1) A boolean `drained` flag …

Then leave the existing mid-`Methods` paragraph intact: it is the source-of-truth rationale and enumerates the sweep cost of a unification, both of which a reader chasing the asymmetry will want, but it no longer carries the burden of being the first place the asymmetry is explained.

Implementer-relevant edge cases:

- Do **not** rename either `drainStateTag` or `tag` as part of this edit; the *Methods* paragraph explicitly defers the unification sweep, which would also touch `initDrainStateTag`, `readDrainState`, the `"loomRegistry.initDrainStateTag"` `details.call` label, and the *Host prerequisites* / *unset tag fallback* prose. The disambiguation hoist is independent and stands alone.
- Preserve the existing mid-`Methods` sentence verbatim (the cross-reference forwards a reader from *Fields* to *Methods* for the full rationale; deleting the original would orphan the cross-reference and lose the sweep-cost enumeration).
- The *idempotent* clause's loose alternation among `snapshot.tag`, "drain-state tag", and `drainStateTag` does not need a sweep — once the *Fields* opener pairs the names, the surrounding prose reads unambiguously.

## Consequence

**Severity:** cosmetic

A first-time reader of the *Fields* paragraph has to scan forward into *Methods* (and past the writer-method bullets) before learning that `drainStateTag` and the snapshot's `tag` key denote the same field. The risk is reader friction rather than incorrect implementation; the field-tuple state space, the three `readDrainState` arms, and the idempotent-predicate semantics are pinned unambiguously elsewhere in the same paragraph.
