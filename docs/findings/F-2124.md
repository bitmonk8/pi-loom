## Finding

GOV-17 (*corpus direction*) defines its two classification terms purely by the presence or absence of a cross-reference *into* the spec corpus from the candidate document's side: "A document or resource outside the spec corpus is a *dependency* of the spec corpus iff its body contains no cross-reference into the spec corpus, and a *dependent* iff it does." The MUST/MUST-NOT pair is then keyed on those terms ("The spec corpus MAY cross-reference its dependencies and MUST NOT cross-reference its dependents").

The predicate diverges from the standard software-engineering reading of the words it reuses. In ordinary usage "A is a dependency of B" means B relies on A — i.e. B references/uses A. GOV-17's predicate never tests whether the spec corpus actually references the document; it tests only whether the *document* links back into the corpus. Two counterintuitive consequences follow directly: (a) a document the spec corpus never mentions is still classified as a "dependency" of the corpus (the rule's own text acknowledges this for external non-Markdown resources, which it calls "vacuously dependencies"); and (b) a document the spec currently references becomes a "dependent" — flipping the corpus's obligation from MAY-link to MUST-NOT-link — the moment the *target* adds a back-link, with no edit on the spec author's side.

The rule is internally consistent and its operational dependent-check is mechanically precise, so an implementer who follows the test verbatim will classify correctly. The defect is a terminology trap: the names "dependency"/"dependent" import a reliance connotation the predicate does not honour, and the definition site never glosses them as bare cross-reference-direction labels. A reader who reasons from the word's standard meaning rather than from the operational test can mis-predict which documents are forbidden cross-reference targets. The `dependency`/`dependent` vocabulary is also reused downstream in the GOV-25–GOV-29 reconciliation on `release-version-naming.md`, so the connotation gap propagates.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

At the GOV-17 definition site, immediately after the sentence defining *dependency*/*dependent*, add a one-sentence gloss that pins the intended semantics and pre-empts the standard-usage misreading. Make three points explicit:

1. The two terms are **cross-reference-direction labels keyed solely on the candidate target's inbound-link presence** — they do **not** assert that the spec corpus relies on, or even references, the document. A document the corpus never cites is still a *dependency* (vacuously).
2. The labels partition **every** document/resource outside the corpus into exactly one of the two classes; *dependency* is the cycle-safe (MAY-link) class and *dependent* is the cycle-forming (MUST-NOT-link) class.
3. A document's class can change **without any edit on the spec author's side** — a *dependency* becomes a *dependent* the moment its own body adds a cross-reference into the corpus (this hook already exists in the *Triggering and scope* paragraph's "standing spec defect" clause; the gloss should forward-reference it so the dynamic-reclassification consequence is visible at the definition).

Keep the existing term names. They are reused in the GOV-25–GOV-29 reconciliation on `release-version-naming.md`, and the precise operational dependent-check already disambiguates application; the gap is purely the missing semantic gloss at the point of definition, so the fix is additive and confined to one paragraph.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

The operational dependent-check is precise, so a careful implementer classifies correctly and no behavioural divergence is forced. The cost is a mental-model trap: an author reasoning from the standard "A relies on B" sense of "dependency" can mislabel a document's cross-reference-direction status and mis-apply GOV-17's MUST-NOT, especially when adding new cross-references to documents the corpus has not previously cited.
