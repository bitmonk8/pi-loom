## Problem

The H4a **Adds** section introduces one artifact — the non-automated, pre-merge real-host smoke — under three distinct bolded definitional terms: **manual real-host smoke governance protocol**, **manual real-host smoke run**, and **required pre-merge gate**. Because bolding is the leaf's convention for introducing a defined term, three bolded phrases for one construct read as three separate artifacts rather than three facets (governance wrapper, concrete run, enforcement status) of one. The downstream consumer — H6a's release-gate acceptance item, which links to H4a — consistently uses **manual real-host smoke run**, so H4a's own definition site does not match the name its consumer settles on.

## Issue introduction

**Verdict:** multi-commit-interaction
**Introducing commits:** 328ba4d — pi-loom plan: resolve "real-host verification gap" (2026-06-10, Thomas Andersen); 15eb44c — pi-loom plan: resolve "real-host divergence pre-merge gate (posture i)" (2026-06-11, Thomas Andersen); ad23856 — pi-loom plan: resolve "H4a manual real-host smoke governance misplaced in Tests" (F-0041+F-0042) (2026-06-16, Thomas Andersen)
**History:** The naming spread accreted across three commits to H4a. `328ba4d` first established the **manual real-host smoke run** name; `15eb44c` added the **required pre-merge gate** framing when it introduced the divergence pre-merge gate; and `ad23856` — which relocated the governance content from Tests into Adds — introduced and bolded the **manual real-host smoke governance protocol** name alongside the two pre-existing ones, leaving all three co-resident as bolded definitional terms in the Adds section.

## Solution approach

Adopt **manual real-host smoke run** as the single canonical bolded defined term in the H4a **Adds** section, matching the name H6a already uses, and demote the other two phrases (governance protocol, required pre-merge gate) to non-definitional descriptors of that run rather than competing bolded terms. Keep the H4a Tests-section back-reference consistent with the chosen canonical term.
