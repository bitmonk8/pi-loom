## Finding

The `LoomRegistry` drain-state contract in `drain-state-contract.md` carries two
claims about the same surface that sit at conflicting normative levels. The
opening sentence of the **`LoomRegistry` drain-state contract** paragraph states
that the registry "exposes a closed four-method call surface … method-call
framing is normative throughout the **`session_shutdown`** handler and step 5
above," and the *Methods* paragraph reinforces that "each method's behavioural
contract … is normative." The four methods are then pinned by exact name and
signature (`LoomRegistry.drain(): void`, `initDrainStateTag(): void`,
`markRuntimeDegraded(): void`, `readDrainState(): {…}`), together with a
property-write prohibition ("no other writer or reader of either field is
permitted").

The same *Methods* paragraph, under the heading *Non-normative editorial
convention*, then declares that this closed-surface restriction "exists for
loom's own maintainability and is **not an observable conformance point**: a
caller cannot distinguish a conformant runtime from one that adds an internal
helper." So one part of the section says the method-call surface and its
property-write prohibition are normative; another part says exactly that surface
is a non-observable internal convention. An implementer reading the contract
cannot tell whether the four exact method names and the no-other-writer/reader
rule are binding conformance requirements or merely an internal-shape convention
they may restructure.

The genuinely binding content — which the fix must preserve — is the *observable*
behavioural contract: the `(drained, tag)` tuple and its permitted transitions,
the three-arm `readDrainState` dispatch and its exhaustiveness (PIC-29), the
no-fourth-arm / no-alternative-gating-field prohibition (PIC-30, observable
through slash-dispatch behaviour), the read-failure fail-safe (PIC-31), and the
effect that `drain()` sets `drained = true` (PIC-32). The named-method surface and
the property-write prohibition are an internal-shape convention the section
itself concedes is unobservable. The defect is that the "method-call framing is
normative" claim is not scoped to the documentation vocabulary, so it reads as
elevating the internal surface to a conformance requirement that the editorial
carve-out simultaneously disclaims.

**History:** `git log -S` traces the two conflicting clauses to different commits. `52b9101` introduced the closed four-method drain-state surface together with the "method-call framing is normative throughout the `session_shutdown` handler" claim (then living in the pre-split monolithic `docs/spec_topics/pi-integration-contract.md`). `git log --diff-filter=A` shows `drain-state-contract.md` was later carved out by the size-cap split `f5e89f4` (2026-06-04 10:38), which moved that clause verbatim without altering it. Fifteen minutes later, `e228dfa` (2026-06-04 10:53) — itself an over-prescription remediation — added the *Non-normative editorial convention* paragraph and the "not an observable conformance point" carve-out (confirmed absent in `e228dfa^`) but did not reconcile the still-present "method-call framing is normative" claim it now contradicts. The defect is therefore the interaction of the two clauses, not a single bad edit: the normative-framing claim was correct in isolation until the later non-observable carve-out landed beside it.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Reconcile the normative level of the named-method surface so the section speaks
with one voice:

- Keep normative: the `(drained, drainStateTag)` field semantics, the three-arm
  `readDrainState` dispatch and its exhaustiveness (PIC-29), the no-fourth-arm /
  no-alternative-gating-field prohibition (PIC-30), the read-failure fail-safe at
  both call sites (PIC-31), and the `drain()` → `drained = true` effect (PIC-32) —
  i.e. everything observable through slash dispatch and teardown behaviour.
- Scope the "method-call framing is normative throughout the `session_shutdown`
  handler" sentence so it explicitly governs only the *documentation vocabulary*
  (the spec describes the handler in method-call terms rather than property-write
  terms; the property-write framing elsewhere names only the throws those methods
  surface). State plainly that the four exact method names and the
  "no other writer or reader of either field is permitted" prohibition are the
  internal-shape editorial convention the same paragraph already declares
  non-observable — not a conformance requirement.

Net effect: a reader sees that conformance is judged on the observable
`(drained, tag)` tuple contract and its dispatch/teardown consequences, while the
exact internal method decomposition is an unobservable convention an
implementation may restructure (consistent with "a caller cannot distinguish a
conformant runtime from one that adds an internal helper"). Watch the four
cross-reference sites that depend on the method names (sub-step 1, the
*Per-step isolation* `details.call` labels, the **idempotent** clause, and the
`reason: "new" | "resume" | "fork"` edge-case bullet): the rename-sweep note that
binds them must be re-expressed as an editorial-consistency note rather than a
normative MUST so it does not re-introduce the same leveling conflict.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

The observable behavioural contract (tuple transitions, three-arm dispatch, fail-
safe) is unambiguous, so an implementer can still produce a working runtime. The
inconsistency leaves the binding status of the four exact method names and the
property-write prohibition undetermined: a conservative implementer may treat the
internal surface as a hard conformance requirement (over-constraining the
implementation the spec elsewhere says is free), while another treats it as
optional. The divergence is in implementation shape, not observable behaviour.
