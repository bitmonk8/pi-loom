## Finding

GOV-15's release-process goal is named with two different root words across the corpus. The owner page is titled `# Source-language stability`, lives at `governance/source-language-stability.md`, anchors the NFR aggregator bullet `<a id="source-language-stability">` labelled `**Source-language stability.**`, and is referenced from `glossary.md`, `language-and-architecture.md`, and `future-considerations/model-changes-and-non-goals.md` by that name. The rule's own normative title and prose, by contrast, consistently use *equivalence*: `**GOV-15 (loom 1.x source-language equivalence — release-process goal).**`, "the equivalence promise", "equivalent under observable (c)", "the source-language-equivalence release goal" (the NFR section's own one-paragraph intro), and the ten-plus cross-page references in `diagnostics/`, `hard-ceilings/`, `req-id-prefix-table-active-b.md`, `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md`, `release-version-naming.md`, and the three plan files (`plan_topics/conventions.md`, `H5e-un-anchored-must-gate.md`, `H6a-live-corpus-activation.md`).

The two words are not synonyms. *Stability* names a release-over-release persistence posture (a goal at the release-process level); *equivalence* names the byte-identical-after-normalisation relation GOV-15's first paragraph actually defines as the operative observable. Today they happen to refer to the same rule, but a reader skimming for "what is the spec promising?" arrives at two different conceptual answers depending on which spelling they land on first. The practical cost is search and reviewer-attention: a `grep` on one form silently omits the other, and the duplication invites future drift where one spelling acquires a qualifier the other doesn't (e.g. the diagnostic-registry carve-out was added only to the "equivalence" prose; the bullet label still reads "Source-language stability").

## Issue introduction

**Verdict:** present-since-inception (multi-commit-interaction, same day)
**Introducing commits:** `6e5e373` (2026-05-06) — added the `**Source-language stability.**` orientation bullet to `spec.md`; `52219c4` (2026-05-06, same day) — coined the rule body as `**GOV-13 (V1.x source-language equivalence — no mechanical gate).**`
**History:** `git log -S "Source-language stability"` and `git log -S "source-language equivalence"` both bottom out on 2026-05-06. The two spellings entered the corpus on the same day in two separate commits — `6e5e373` introduced the orientation bullet under the "stability" word, while `52219c4` (later the same day) introduced the rule body itself under the "equivalence" word — so the dual naming has existed for the entire life of GOV-15's predecessor (GOV-13) and of GOV-15 itself. Subsequent migrations preserved the split intact: `c253233` (2026-05-07) renamed GOV-13→GOV-15 keeping the "equivalence" wording in the rule body; `7995d3c` (2026-06-05) moved the rule body off `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md` into a new owner page and named that page `source-language-stability.md` / `# Source-language stability` — choosing the orientation-bullet spelling for the file/heading while leaving the rule's normative title and prose on the "equivalence" spelling, cementing the two-name pattern. No commit ever attempted to unify them.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** multiple

### Option A — Adopt "equivalence" everywhere

**Approach.** Rename the owner page to `source-language-equivalence.md` and re-anchor its H1 / aggregator-bullet anchor / aggregator-bullet label / all cross-page reference labels to use *equivalence*. Update the TOC entry on `governance.md`, the glossary entry, the `language-and-architecture.md` top-of-file comment, the `future-considerations/model-changes-and-non-goals.md` entry and `Recorded at:` line, the `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md` HTML comment, the two plan leaves `H5e`/`H6a`, and the two plan support files `conventions.md`/`coverage-matrix.md`.

**Spec edits.** Single search-and-replace pass across the 13 spec files + 3 plan/plan-support files listed in Spec Documents and Plan Impact. The diagnostic-registry-carve-out language and per-page lock-step references all already use "equivalence" prose, so the asymmetry vanishes naturally.

**Pros.** Aligns with the word GOV-15's normative title and prose already use throughout (the dominant spelling by count: ≈30 occurrences vs ≈10 for "stability"). Eliminates the "stability vs equivalence" reader confusion at the root, since *equivalence* is the operative observable the rule actually defines.

**Cons.** Wider link-target churn: the file path / anchor target / aggregator-bullet anchor `#source-language-stability` all change, which touches every inbound cross-reference. The orientation bullet *label* changes from a familiar word ("stability") to a more technical one ("equivalence").

**Risks.** A stale link to `#source-language-stability` in any reviewer's local notes or PR description will silently 404 after the rename; mitigated by leaving a one-line stub anchor (`<a id="source-language-stability"></a>`) on the renamed bullet for the loom 1.x window.

### Option B — Adopt "stability" everywhere

**Approach.** Rename GOV-15's title to `**GOV-15 (loom 1.x source-language stability — release-process goal).**` and rewrite the rule's prose to consistently say "stability promise" / "stability relation" / "the stability set" wherever it currently says "equivalence". Leave file path, anchor, and aggregator-bullet label untouched.

**Spec edits.** Concentrated on `source-language-stability.md` (one page, many in-page occurrences) plus the cross-page reference labels that read "source-language equivalence release-process goal" or "source-language-equivalence reviewer-inspection step" (re-label only; link targets are already on the `source-language-stability` anchor).

**Pros.** No file/anchor renames, so inbound links and existing plan-side references continue to resolve without modification. Keeps the friendly NFR-bullet label.

**Cons.** Loses the technical precision of the word *equivalence* — the rule defines a byte-identical-after-normalisation relation, which is exactly what "equivalence" denotes and is not what "stability" conventionally means. Re-wording GOV-15's body to drop "equivalence" wherever it currently appears risks losing the connection to the placeholder-rendering categories' equivalence terminology, which `diagnostics/placeholder-rendering-a.md` explicitly anchors on.

**Risks.** Future readers may re-introduce "equivalence" when extending GOV-15 because the rule's content genuinely is about an equivalence relation; the dual-naming defect could silently reappear.

### Option C — Keep both terms; pin distinct senses

**Approach.** Treat "stability" and "equivalence" as deliberately distinct terms and glossary-pin each: *stability* names the release-over-release persistence posture (the umbrella, what the NFR-bullet promises); *equivalence* names the specific observable relation (byte-identity after normalisation) that GOV-15 defines as the test for stability. Add a glossary entry for each and a synonym cross-reference, and audit every existing call-site to use the term that fits its sentence.

**Spec edits.** Add two `glossary.md` entries (`source-language stability`, `source-language equivalence`). Audit and adjust the existing 40-odd call-sites so each one uses the term that matches its local meaning (most current "equivalence" sites stay; aggregator bullets and the file/heading naming the disposition stay on "stability"). No file/anchor renames.

**Pros.** Both words carry useful, distinguishable meaning; preserving the split makes that distinction explicit instead of implicit. Smallest link-target churn.

**Cons.** Adds a new pair of glossary-governed terms whose distinction maintainers must internalise; the audit pass is judgement-laden ("which term fits here?") and harder to do mechanically than a global rename. Introduces a second governed-identifier pair that GOV-30 lock-step must thereafter track.

**Risks.** The distinction is subtle enough that future contributors may drift back to using the two interchangeably; the audit needs a follow-up review at every GOV-15 amendment.

### Recommendation

Take **Option A** (adopt *equivalence* everywhere). The dominant existing usage is already on "equivalence" (the rule title, the rule prose, ~30 cross-page references), the word matches the operative observable GOV-15 actually defines, and the rename eliminates the dual-naming surface in a single mechanical pass rather than asking every future maintainer to police it. Edge cases the implementer must watch: (i) leave a `<a id="source-language-stability"></a>` alias anchor on the renamed bullet so loom-1.x stale links keep resolving; (ii) the file path change `source-language-stability.md` → `source-language-equivalence.md` must update all relative-link targets in the 13 spec files and 4 plan files (`H5e`, `H6a`, `conventions.md`, `coverage-matrix.md`) in the same commit per GOV-30 lock-step; (iii) the GOV-14 editorial note on `req-id-prefix-table-active-b.md`, the `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md` HTML-comment provenance record, and the glossary `GOV-N` entry already use "equivalence" wording and need only the link-target update; (iv) preserve the `#gov-15`, `#gov-15-loads-cleanly`, `#gov-15-fixture-suite`, `#ceiling-set-carve-out`, `#diagnostic-registry-carve-out`, `#attribution-test`, and `#operational-definitions` sub-anchors verbatim — they are the bulk of inbound link traffic and are spelling-neutral.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

Implementers can still produce a working system — runtime behaviour does not turn on the spelling. The cost is borne by reviewers and corpus maintainers: a search on one root word silently omits the other (across ≈15 spec files and 3 plan files), and the asymmetric distribution of qualifiers between the two spellings (the diagnostic-registry carve-out is attached to "equivalence" prose; the orientation bullet still reads "Source-language stability") creates a slow-moving drift surface that future GOV-15 amendments must remember to keep in lock-step manually.
