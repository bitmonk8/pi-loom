## Finding

The `*Recognised import/access shapes.*` paragraph in `docs/spec_topics/pi-integration-contract/audit-target-categories.md` introduces the long enumeration of family-(4) shapes (`import * as pi`, default imports, dynamic `import()`, side-effect-only imports, CJS reach, computed access, namespace destructuring, aliased rebindings, captured rebindings, off-canonical carriers, etc.) with the phrase "Other syntactic shapes that fall under the same behavioural rule — …". By that point in the paragraph the reader has been shown at least four candidate "rules" the antecedent could refer to: (i) the umbrella "barrel-module" prohibition; (ii) the `export *` / `export * as ns` prohibition, which is itself self-described as "a behavioural rule on the source-text shape rather than a tooling-implementation constraint"; (iii) the per-symbol `export { … } from "@earendil-works/<peer>"` permission rule that immediately precedes; (iv) the same-line `// allow-pi-surface:` exemption-marker scope rule that is the actual immediate left-neighbour. Only candidate (ii) re-uses the words "behavioural rule" and is plausibly what the author meant, but the construction "the same behavioural rule" — definite article, no anchor, no quoted phrase — leaves an implementer free to key on any of the four.

The downstream consequence is small because every enumerated shape is then *explicitly* routed in-paragraph to "family (4) under its own discriminator", so the resolution path is pinned regardless of which antecedent the reader picks. The defect is editorial: the prose claims a unifying rule, declines to name it, and so reads as hand-waving in a paragraph whose entire register is exhaustively literal.

The fix names the rule directly — e.g. "subject to the same source-text-shape prohibition as `export * from "@earendil-works/<peer>"`, routed to family (4) under its own discriminator" — so the back-reference resolves syntactically against the `export *` clause without the reader having to reconstruct intent.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Replace the opening clause "Other syntactic shapes that fall under the same behavioural rule —" with a clause that names the antecedent explicitly. Concretely, swap in:

> "Other syntactic shapes subject to the same source-text-shape prohibition as `export * from "@earendil-works/<peer>"` above — routed to family (4) under its own discriminator on the same footing as `export *` —"

This (a) anchors the back-reference to the only earlier clause that calls itself a "behavioural rule on the source-text shape" and (b) front-loads the family-(4) routing that the sentence redundantly re-pins for each enumerated shape further on, so a reader who skims the opener still leaves with the correct discriminator. No structural change to the enumeration itself; the trailing per-shape "the prohibition is in family (4) under its own discriminator on the same footing as …" clauses stay as authored.

Edge case for the implementer: the substitution removes the words "behavioural rule" from this sentence; do not also remove them from the `export *` clause above (which uses them to draw the source-text-shape-vs-tooling-implementation distinction the new wording leans on).

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

A purely editorial gap: the family-(4) routing for each enumerated shape is pinned later in the same sentence, so an implementer who picks the wrong antecedent still routes the shapes correctly. The cost is reader friction in an already-dense paragraph and a small invitation to mis-cite "the behavioural rule" in downstream prose, leaf descriptions, or test names.
