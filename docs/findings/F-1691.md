## Finding

PIC-52 ("No additional access channels") packs two normative obligations of different verification character into one block. The first — "The runtime MUST NOT introduce additional access channels (sandbox, capability filter, mediated proxy) in any loom 1.x release; widening or narrowing this rule is a major-version concern" — is a design-time architectural prohibition. It constrains how the runtime may be built, not what it does at run time: the *absence* of an access channel is not a state any conformance test can observe, so the MUST is verifiable only by code review / architectural inspection. The second obligation — "silent success on denial is forbidden," with host-side denials mapped to `Err(QueryError { kind: "code_tool", cause: "execution", ... })` — is a concrete runtime behaviour a test can witness on the tool-execution failure path.

Bundling the two under one undifferentiated MUST leaves the verification mode of each implicit. A test author reading PIC-52 cannot tell that the channel-prohibition is review-verified and only the denial-routing clause is runtime-testable; they may either attempt (impossible) test coverage of the prohibition, or treat the whole rule as untestable and skip the part that is. The plan corroborates the split in practice: the two leaves that cite PIC-52 (`V14d`, `V14d-T`) pin their Tests and Ships-when exclusively to the silent-success-on-denial observable and say nothing about the channel prohibition — i.e. the testable half is the only half the plan can act on, while the prohibition rides along with no verification anchor.

This is a structural/verification-clarity defect, not a correctness defect: the behaviour the rule mandates (no interposed privilege layer; denials surface as `Err`) is unambiguous. The fix is to make the two obligations' verification modes explicit rather than to change what the runtime does.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** multiple

### Option A — Retain the prohibition as normative; separate and label the two obligations by verification mode

**Approach:** Keep "MUST NOT introduce additional access channels … major-version concern" as a normative MUST, but split PIC-52's body so the architectural prohibition and the runtime-testable denial-routing clause are distinct, each with its own sub-anchor, and annotate the prohibition as review/architecture-verified (not runtime-testable) while the denial clause carries the conformance-test obligation.

**Spec edits:** In `trust-boundary.md`, break the single paragraph into two anchored obligations: (1) the design invariant (no interposed privilege layer; MUST NOT add sandbox/capability-filter/mediated-proxy channels in 1.x; major-version concern) tagged as verified by architectural review, and (2) the denial-routing observable (`Err(QueryError{kind:"code_tool", cause:"execution"})`; silent success forbidden) as the runtime-testable MUST. Repoint `V14d`/`V14d-T` to the new denial-routing sub-anchor.

**Pros:** Preserves the security-relevant prohibition's normative force and its "major-version concern" governance; resolves the testability concern by making the verification mode explicit; gives the plan a precise anchor for the half it actually tests.

**Cons:** Introduces a verification-mode convention (review-verified vs test-verified) that the spec may not otherwise establish; slightly longer rule.

**Risks:** If the spec corpus has no existing notion of "review-verified MUST," this edit implicitly coins one; confirm consistency with how other architectural MUSTs (e.g. trust-boundary siblings) are framed.

### Option B — Demote the prohibition to a non-normative design principle

**Approach:** Reword "MUST NOT introduce additional access channels … major-version concern" as a non-normative design statement (e.g. "By design, the runtime interposes no privilege layer between loom code and the Pi extension host"), and keep "silent success on denial is forbidden" as the sole normative, testable observable.

**Spec edits:** In `trust-boundary.md`, replace the MUST-NOT sentence with a descriptive design-principle sentence; leave the denial-routing clause as the lone normative MUST.

**Pros:** Removes the untestable MUST entirely, exactly satisfying the testability lens; smallest conceptual surface (one normative observable).

**Cons:** Strips binding force from a security-relevant architectural invariant and discards the "major-version concern" governance hook; a future 1.x change that adds a mediated proxy would no longer violate a normative rule.

**Risks:** Loss of the no-privilege-layer guarantee as an enforceable constraint; downstream cross-links that treat PIC-52 as a normative anchor (e.g. PIC-17 active-set gating references) may now point at a non-normative statement.

### Recommendation

Take **Option A**. The channel prohibition is a deliberate, security-bearing architectural invariant with explicit major-version governance; demoting it (Option B) would surrender that for a verification-form concern that is better resolved by making the verification mode explicit. Splitting the block into a review-verified design invariant and a runtime-testable denial observable removes the ambiguity the finding identifies while keeping both obligations binding. Implementer watch-points: ensure `V14d`/`V14d-T` repoint to the new denial-routing sub-anchor, and confirm the "review-verified" framing matches how sibling trust-boundary requirements are expressed before coining a new convention.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

A test author or auditor cannot derive a verification path from PIC-52's first sentence and gets no signal that it is review-verified by design; the risk is either wasted effort attempting to test an unobservable property or, conversely, dismissing the entire rule (including the testable denial-routing clause) as unverifiable. No two implementers diverge on runtime behaviour, so a working system is still producible.
