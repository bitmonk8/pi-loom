## Finding

The closing clause of the long enumeration of prohibited shapes in `audit-target-categories.md` reads:

> `Object.assign({}, pi)`, `keyof typeof pi`, and re-exports through barrel modules — **are out of scope of inventory-resolution and MUST NOT be used** on the `pi` namespace, on the canonical `ctx` identifier, or against any of the four peer packages anywhere in the audit's scope.

In spec usage, "out of scope" idiomatically means "the artefact under discussion does not cover this case." Pairing it with "MUST NOT be used" on the same clause produces an immediately contradictory reading: the page is simultaneously saying "the audit need not address these shapes" and "these shapes are prohibited." An implementer reading the paragraph idiomatically — and the paragraph runs for several thousand words, so paragraph-end reading is realistic — could plausibly conclude that the family-(4) shapes are merely declarative prose with no audit-side detection obligation.

The next sentence ("The audit itself MUST enforce that prohibition under its own discriminator (see *Failure-surface contract* below)") does reinstate the enforcement obligation, so a careful reader who continues past the en-dash recovers the intent. But the misreading window exists on a normative MUST-bearing clause that establishes the entire family-(4) discriminator — the only non-exemptible audit family, and the one `V18b` cites verbatim as "the non-exemptible family-(4) out-of-scope import/access discriminator." The phrasing also leaks into downstream callsites: the family is *named* `out-of-scope import/access` (in plan leaves and the coverage matrix), which inherits the same exclusion-connotation defect.

The defect is that "out of scope of inventory-resolution" conflates two distinct properties: (i) these shapes do not have a resolution target in `SDK_SURFACE_INVENTORY` (true — they launder the canonical name and so no entry can resolve them), and (ii) they are out of scope of the audit (false — they are precisely *in* scope of the audit, under family (4)). Property (i) is the *reason* for the prohibition; the prose needs to surface (i) without inheriting the connotation of (ii).

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Rewrite the closing clause to surface the *unresolvability* property as the reason for the prohibition, dropping the "out of scope" idiom. Use a phrasing along the lines of:

> `Object.assign({}, pi)`, `keyof typeof pi`, and re-exports through barrel modules — **cannot be resolved against `SDK_SURFACE_INVENTORY`** (the canonical-name resolution path on which the category-(1)/(2)/(3) join keys depend has no fixed point for these shapes) **and MUST NOT be used** on the `pi` namespace, on the canonical `ctx` identifier, or against any of the four peer packages anywhere in the audit's scope. The audit MUST detect every occurrence of these shapes and route it under the family-(4) discriminator (see *Failure-surface contract* below); an `// allow-pi-surface:` marker MUST NOT silence a family-(4) record.

Implementer-relevant edge cases for the rewriter to keep intact while making this edit:

- The closing clause is the head of a sentence whose tail (*"The audit itself MUST enforce that prohibition under its own discriminator…"*) is the existing enforcement obligation. The rewrite must preserve that obligation rather than replacing it; combining the two into a single sentence is acceptable provided both the prohibition and the detection MUST survive.
- The clause is the closure of a multi-thousand-word enumeration of family-(4) shapes (`import * as pi`, dynamic `import()`, CJS reach via `createRequire`, computed access, captured rebindings, off-canonical-name / off-canonical-annotation `ctx` / `pi` parameters, generic-laundering type parameters, subtype-creation declarations, non-parameter carrier bindings, etc.). The closing clause's grammatical scope is the *entire* enumeration, not only the three shapes (`Object.assign({}, pi)`, `keyof typeof pi`, barrel-module re-exports) named immediately before the em-dash; the rewrite must not narrow the scope. The MUST-detect obligation introduced by the rewrite therefore covers every shape in the enumeration above the closing clause.
- The non-exemptibility of family-(4) markers (`// allow-pi-surface:` does not silence them) is currently asserted in `V18b` and `audit-failures.md` but is *not* asserted at this clause in `audit-target-categories.md`. The rewrite is a natural place to add it as the third conjunct (prohibition + detection + non-exemptibility), closing a gap currently distributed across other pages.
- The family is named "out-of-scope import/access" in `V18b` / coverage matrix prose and (if so) in `audit-failures.md`'s *Failure-surface contract*. The rewrite of this clause does not by itself rename the family; if the family's discriminator name is to be changed (e.g. to `unresolvable import/access`), that is a coordinated rename across `audit-failures.md`, `V18b-inventory-audit.md`, `V18b-T-inventory-audit.md`, and `coverage-matrix.md` and is out of scope of this finding. Leaving the discriminator name unchanged is acceptable; the clause-level rewrite resolves the immediate misreading regardless.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

A first-reading implementer could conclude that family-(4) shapes are descriptive prohibitions only, without audit-side detection — directly contradicting `V18b`'s non-exemptible family-(4) discriminator. The next sentence reinstates the obligation, so a thorough reader recovers; the risk is primarily that the family's prose name and its defining clause both carry an exclusion connotation on the spec's only non-exemptible audit family.
