## Finding

The `V15f` (and identical `V15f-T`) arity Tests bullet reads: "more than the total `params:` count is always `invoke-arity-too-many`, even when the callee is not statically resolvable." Read in isolation this is ambiguous on two axes the implementer must pin to write the test:

- **What count is compared, and against what, when the callee is not statically resolvable.** Arity-too-many is defined relative to "the total `params:` count" — a fact read from the callee. But "not statically resolvable" is exactly the case where the callee could not be opened/parsed/lowered (per `invocation.md` §Static resolution), so the leaf appears to require comparing against a count that, on its face, is unavailable in that case.
- **The phase.** The sibling arity-too-few clause in the same bullet explicitly splits parse-time (statically resolvable) from runtime AJV fallback (not statically resolvable). The too-many clause asserts "always" without restating the parse-time anchor, so a reader can plausibly take "always" to mean "fires at runtime once resolved" rather than "always at parse time."

The authoritative source, `invocation.md` §Argument arity, resolves both: it states the too-many case is "**Always a parse error** `loom/parse/invoke-arity-too-many`, even when the callee is not statically resolvable: extra positional arguments cannot be matched to any param, and there is no runtime safety net possible." That rationale clause — the contrast with too-few (which *does* have a runtime AJV net for missing required fields, hence defers) and the explicit "no runtime safety net possible" — is the text that pins phase to parse-time and explains the asymmetry. The `V15f`/`V15f-T` bullet dropped it, so the leaf is strictly less precise than the spec it implements.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

In the `V15f` arity Tests bullet — and the byte-identical bullet in `V15f-T` — restore the disambiguating content the leaf dropped from `invocation.md` §Argument arity's "Too many arguments" bullet, so the leaf is as precise as the spec it implements. The arity-too-many clause should carry:

- the explicit parse-time anchor: more than the callee's total `params:` count is **always a parse error** `loom/parse/invoke-arity-too-many`;
- the "even when the callee is not statically resolvable" qualifier together with the spec's rationale for it — surplus positional arguments have no param slot to bind to, so no runtime AJV check can catch them (which is why too-many cannot defer to runtime the way too-few does);
- the contrast that makes the asymmetry legible: arity-too-few defers to the runtime AJV missing-required-field check when the callee is not statically resolvable, whereas arity-too-many has no such net and is therefore decided at parse time.

The literal text should mirror `invocation.md` §Argument arity's "Too many arguments" bullet. `invocation.md` is read-only for this fix; the leaf reproduces the spec's existing normative claim rather than coining a new one.

Edge case for the implementer: do not invent a redefinition of "statically resolvable" for the arity check — the leaf's obligation is to assert the same behaviour the spec mandates. The residual question the spec text itself leaves implicit — by what mechanism the parser obtains the callee's `params:` count for a callee that is "not statically resolvable" — is a spec-fidelity matter for the spec-review track, not a plan edit; the plan leaf must not resolve it by diverging from `invocation.md`.

## Consequence

**Severity:** correctness

An implementer writing the too-many arm cannot determine, for a not-statically-resolvable callee, what count the parse check consults or how to construct the triggering fixture. Two reasonable implementers diverge — one builds a callee that parses far enough to expose `params:` but fails deeper structural checks and asserts the parse error there; another concludes the assertion is unconstructible (no `params:` count to compare) and omits the arm or moves it to runtime — and the `V15f` and `V15f-T` test sets can disagree on what behaviour they assert.
