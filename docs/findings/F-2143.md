## Finding

The `LoomRegistry` drain-state contract refers to one underlying value under two names. The internal field is `drainStateTag` (TypeScript type `"shutting-down" | "degraded-needs-reload" | undefined`), while the snapshot key returned by `readDrainState()` is the shorter `tag` — so dispatch sites read `snapshot.tag === "shutting-down"` rather than `snapshot.drainStateTag === "shutting-down"`. The asymmetry is deliberate and the spec does state it: the *Methods* paragraph carries the bridge sentence "The internal field is named `drainStateTag`; the public snapshot key on `readDrainState`'s return value is the shorter `tag`. The rename is intentional … and the two names refer to the same field."

The defect is placement, not omission. That bridge sentence sits mid-*Methods*, after the *Fields* subsection has already introduced `drainStateTag` and after the writer-method bullets (`initDrainStateTag`, `markRuntimeDegraded`) have used it. A reader who works through *Fields* and then jumps to the `LoomRegistry.readDrainState` bullet to learn the dispatch API meets `drainStateTag` (field, writers) and `tag` (snapshot signature, PIC-29's `snapshot.tag` arms) in use *before* encountering the sentence that tells them the two denote one field. The first place the names appear paired is several paragraphs after both are already load-bearing.

The same asymmetry then reads loosely through neighbouring prose: the *idempotent* clause and PIC-29 alternate among `snapshot.tag`, the bare hyphenated noun "drain-state tag" (the field in the abstract), and the literal `drainStateTag`. None of these is wrong, but a reader who has not yet reached the mid-*Methods* bridge has no upfront signal that the snapshot mirrors the field rather than carrying a derived value.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Hoist a one-sentence pairing to the head of the *Fields* subsection, immediately as the field tuple is introduced and before `drainStateTag` is first used by a writer bullet, so a reader meets both names paired before either carries a contract. Concrete form:

> *Fields.* The contract carries two fields, exposed to readers as the snapshot tuple `{ drained, tag }` that `readDrainState()` returns — `tag` is the snapshot key for the field the rest of this section names `drainStateTag`; the rename is intentional and unifying it is out of scope for loom 1.0 (see *Methods* below for the full rationale and sweep cost). (1) A boolean `drained` flag …

Then leave the existing mid-*Methods* bridge sentence intact as the source-of-truth rationale; it enumerates the sibling names a unification would have to sweep, which a reader chasing the asymmetry will want, but it no longer carries the burden of being the first place the asymmetry is explained.

Implementer-relevant edge cases:

- Do **not** rename either `drainStateTag` or `tag`. The *Methods* paragraph explicitly defers that unification as out of scope for loom 1.0; it would also have to sweep `initDrainStateTag`, `readDrainState`, the `"loomRegistry.initDrainStateTag"` `details.call` label, and the *Host prerequisites* / *unset tag fallback* prose. The disambiguation hoist is independent and stands alone.
- Preserve the existing mid-*Methods* sentence verbatim so the new *Fields* pointer's "see *Methods* below" cross-reference resolves; deleting it would orphan the cross-reference and lose the sweep-cost enumeration.
- The *idempotent* clause / PIC-29 alternation among `snapshot.tag`, "drain-state tag", and `drainStateTag` needs no sweep — once the *Fields* opener pairs the names, the surrounding prose reads unambiguously.

## Consequence

**Severity:** cosmetic

A first-time reader of the *Fields* subsection must scan forward past the writer-method bullets before learning that `drainStateTag` and the snapshot's `tag` key denote the same field. The risk is reader friction, not divergent implementation: the field-tuple state space, the three `readDrainState` arms, and the idempotent-predicate semantics are pinned unambiguously in the same paragraph.
