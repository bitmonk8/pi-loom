## Finding

The single `H3a` identifier-keyed architectural scan that flags direct ambient-primitive references in `src/**` is referred to under two different adjectival names in `conventions.md`. The dominant spelling, **"ambient-primitive scan"**, appears four times — in the §Cross-cutting-rules scope paragraph, in the residue-inspection intro, and in checklist items 8 and 9. The §REQ-ID-discipline section instead spells it **"ambient-access scan"** ("Like the H3a identifier-keyed ambient-access scan, it catches only what the token scan witnesses"). A reader meeting both spellings cannot immediately tell they denote one and the same `H3a` scan.

The same drift affects the exemption-comment set. The §No-globals-statics-singletons rule names it **"the ambient-access allow-list"**, whereas the canonical registered term (per the review index's terms registry) is **"ambient-primitive allow-list"**. The two-name split means a search or naming-consistency audit keyed on one spelling silently misses the other.

The drift has also propagated into two leaf bodies: both `H4a` and `M` describe keeping "the `H3a` ambient-access scan" clean, while the owning convention's dominant spelling is "ambient-primitive scan". (A third descriptor, "identifier-keyed scan", is used in §REQ-ID discipline, the residue checklist, and the `H3a`/`V8d` leaves; that phrase names the scan's *mechanism* rather than its subject and is internally consistent, so it is not part of this defect.)

## Issue introduction

**Verdict:** multi-commit-interaction
**Introducing commits:** `1035d0b` ("un-anchored-MUST closing-gate recogniser overclaims precision/recall", 2026-06-10) introduced "ambient-access scan" in §REQ-ID discipline; `49379da` ("ambient-access scan seam-adapter recognition rule", 2026-06-11) introduced "ambient-access allow-list" in §No-globals; `4b3c2c0` (2026-06-17), `2f18db6`, and `1c9ede1` (2026-06-18) introduced and expanded the "ambient-primitive scan" spelling in the scope paragraph and residue-inspection items without reconciling the surviving "ambient-access" occurrences.
**History:** No single commit introduced an internally inconsistent state. The "ambient-access" spellings landed first and were locally coherent; the later "ambient-primitive scan" edits standardised most occurrences but left the §REQ-ID-discipline "ambient-access scan" and the §No-globals "ambient-access allow-list" untouched, so the inconsistency emerged from the interaction of the earlier and later edits rather than from any one of them.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Standardise the scan's subject adjective on **"ambient-primitive"** across the corpus:

- In `conventions.md` §No globals, statics, singletons, change the phrase *the ambient-access allow-list* to *the ambient-primitive allow-list* (the term the review index already registers as canonical).
- In `conventions.md` §REQ-ID discipline, replace the sole "ambient-access scan" occurrence ("Like the H3a identifier-keyed ambient-access scan, it catches only what the token scan witnesses") with "ambient-primitive scan".
- In `H4a-factory-shell-and-harness.md` Adds, replace "keeping the `H3a` ambient-access scan clean" with "...ambient-primitive scan clean".
- In `M-minimal-slash-command.md` Adds, replace "the `H3a` ambient-access scan stays clean" with "...ambient-primitive scan stays clean".

Leave the "identifier-keyed scan" mechanism descriptor as-is — it names how the scan works, not its subject, and is used consistently. Likewise the "ambient-access ban" / "no ambient access" phrasing in `H3a`/`V8b` describes the prohibition rather than the scan, and is outside this rename.

## Consequence

**Severity:** cosmetic

A reader scanning for one spelling misses occurrences under the other, and a naming-consistency check keyed on "ambient-primitive allow-list" does not reconcile the §No-globals "ambient-access allow-list" usage. Execution is unaffected — all variants are anchored to `H3a`, so the referent is recoverable — but the drift weakens the corpus's single-name discipline and risks future divergence.
