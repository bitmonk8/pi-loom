## Finding

DISC-6 derives the per-candidate `package.json` read deadline as
`deadline = max(200, floor(looms.scanPackagesTimeoutMs / 10))` milliseconds and
glosses the intent as "so the default `2000 ms` global cap yields a `200 ms`
per-read deadline; raising the global cap automatically raises the per-read
budget." The gloss explains the *proportional behaviour* but not the two specific
numeric choices: why the divisor is `10` (rather than `5` or `20`) and why the
lower bound is `200 ms`. Both are presented as normative, derived, and
non-configurable, yet the spec gives no reason a reader can use to judge whether
the values are load-bearing or arbitrary.

This sits awkwardly against DISC-6's own framing, which justifies making the two
global caps operator-tunable "rather than hardcoded" precisely because install
characteristics vary — while silently hardcoding two further derivation constants
with no accompanying reasoning. An implementer or maintainer cannot tell whether
the `1/10` ratio and `200 ms` floor encode a deliberate invariant (e.g. bound any
one read to a fraction of the whole walk; keep the floor above realistic local
read latency) or are placeholder values open to tuning.

The formula is deliberately pinned exactly because the `V10b`/`V10b-T` package-walk
conformance tests assert the per-read trip point through the `FakeClock` seam; the
gap is not the precision but the absence of a stated reason for the constants.

## Issue introduction

**Verdict:** multi-commit-interaction
**Introducing commits:** `25e8a60` (2026-05-07, "pi-loom spec: resolve \"T08 — Per-package.json read timeout: overrun is unbounded\""), `cb82bbf` (2026-06-01, "pi-loom spec: resolve \"Settings scalar keys lack a malformed / out-of-range rule\"")
**History:** The per-read formula `deadline = max(200, floor(looms.scanPackagesTimeoutMs / 10))` was introduced by `25e8a60` when the spec resolved an earlier "overrun is unbounded" finding by wrapping each candidate read in a `Promise.race` against a derived deadline. At that point no lower bound on `looms.scanPackagesTimeoutMs` was documented, so the `max(200, …)` floor was implicit defence against pathologically small caps and the contradiction was latent. The acceptance set "`looms.scanPackagesTimeoutMs` — an integer ≥ 1" was added later by `cb82bbf` when "Settings scalar keys lack a malformed / out-of-range rule" was resolved; that commit established a per-key validation table without re-auditing the per-read formula, and the gap between "≥ 1 is accepted" and "per-read floor is `200`" became observable. Neither commit on its own is wrong — the defect is the interaction between the derived per-read formula and the independently-authored acceptance bound.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Add a brief rationale clause to the DISC-6 per-read-deadline sentence, immediately
after the existing proportionality gloss, stating what the two constants buy.
Concretely, explain that:

- the `1/10` divisor bounds any single candidate read to roughly a tenth of the
  whole-walk budget, so no small handful of slow reads can dominate the walk and
  the global `loom/load/discovery-slow` cap stays the dominant control; and
- the `200 ms` floor keeps the per-read deadline well above realistic local
  `package.json` read latency (sub-millisecond to low-millisecond on a healthy
  filesystem), so a small global cap does not abort every read prematurely.

Keep the formula and its "derived, not configurable in loom 1.0" status exactly as
written so the `FakeClock`-driven `V10b`/`V10b-T` conformance assertions remain
valid. Do not relax the normative text to a bare "monotonically derived" property:
that would strip the deterministic trip point those tests assert.

Implementer edge case: the floor's behaviour when an operator sets
`looms.scanPackagesTimeoutMs` below `200` is governed by the separate per-read /
global-timeout ordering rule already stated in DISC-6; the new rationale clause
should be phrased so it does not contradict that ordering.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

Nothing breaks at runtime and no two implementers diverge — the formula is exact
and testable. The cost is a guidance gap: a reader cannot tell whether `10` and
`200` are deliberate invariants or arbitrary, which invites either misplaced
tuning or unwarranted reverence, and contradicts DISC-6's stated preference for
explaining its bounds rather than hardcoding them.
